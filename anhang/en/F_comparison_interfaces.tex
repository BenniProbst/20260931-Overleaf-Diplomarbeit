\chapter{Comparison Interfaces: \texttt{std::map} and \texttt{std::vector}}\label{app:interfaces}

% [AP-G5-Anhang / AP-EN] Mirror of anhang/de/F_comparison_interfaces.tex. Full standard interface tables (C++23).
This appendix presents --- complementing Section~\ref{sec:compare-interfaces} --- the complete listing of all
standard interface functions of the two comparison shells \texttt{std::map} (ordered associative) and
\texttt{std::vector} (sequence) as of C++23, with their officially expected semantics and complexity
guarantee per the ISO C++ standard~\cite{iso_cpp}. It also serves as a central reference for the further
development of the cache engine: because the search-algorithm shell replaces its internals with dynamically
interchangeable axis algorithms (Section~\ref{ssec:dynamics-levels}), it becomes explanation-worthy, per
standard function, what behaviour the external contract guarantees.

\emph{Variadic mapping.} One template parameter provides the \texttt{std::vector} API, two parameters the
\texttt{std::map} API, and $N>2$ a \texttt{std::map<K,\,std::tuple<\ldots>>}
(Section~\ref{sec:stdmap-interface}); the shared functions (iteration,
\texttt{size}/\texttt{empty}/\texttt{clear}, \texttt{swap}, \texttt{get\_allocator}) carry the same semantics
in both shells.

\emph{Composite functions.} Some functions arise ``under the hood'' as a composition of more primitive
operations --- e.g.\ \texttt{operator[]} as \texttt{lower\_bound} followed by default insertion on a missing
key, or \texttt{at} as \texttt{find} with a thrown \texttt{std::out\_of\_range} exception on absence ---; such
cases are marked in the ``Note'' column. The ``Since'' column names the introducing standard version. The
exact cache-engine-internal function-handle hops are documented in a dedicated cache-engine developer
document.

\emph{Conformance subset.} The conformance gate (Section~\ref{ssec:measurement-patterns}) checks a core
subset of the \texttt{std::map} operations before each measurement, so that only comparable things are
compared; it is marked as such in the \texttt{std::map} table.

\noindent The specialization \texttt{std::vector<bool>} packs boolean values bitwise: it has no
\texttt{data()} and no contiguity guarantee, returns a proxy type \texttt{reference} instead of
\texttt{bool\&} via \texttt{operator[]}/\allowbreak\texttt{at}/\allowbreak\texttt{front}/\allowbreak\texttt{back}, and additionally offers
\texttt{flip()} (at the vector and the \texttt{reference} level), a static
\texttt{swap(reference,\allowbreak\ reference)}, and a \texttt{std::hash} specialization. The comparison operators behave as for \texttt{std::map}.

\section{Composite Functions and the Conformance Subset}\label{asec:if-decomp}
Several interface functions are ``under the hood'' compositions of more primitive operations
(Table~\ref{tab:if-decomp}). For this thesis that is the central observation: all map accessors and modifiers
rest on a single ordered-search primitive (\texttt{lower\_bound}/\texttt{find}); an axis implementation
therefore only needs to provide a correct ordered search plus node linking, and the rest follows as a fixed
composition --- exactly the substitutability whose comparison is the central measurement act
(Section~\ref{sec:stdmap-interface}). The conformance gate checks the core subset marked with $\dagger$ in
Table~\ref{tab:if-map}.

\caption{Composite functions (``under the hood'')}\label{tab:if-decomp}\\

\midrule
\endfirsthead
\toprule

\midrule
\endhead
\texttt{map::}\allowbreak\texttt{operator[]} & \texttt{lower\_bound} + default insertion on absence $\to$ reference & mutates on absence; never throws \\
\texttt{map::at} & \texttt{find} + throw \texttt{std::out\_of\_range} on absence & the only throwing access; never mutates \\
\texttt{map::insert} / \texttt{emplace} & ordered search + link a node only if the key is absent & no overwrite; \texttt{emplace} may still consume arguments on a hit \\
\texttt{map::try\_\allowbreak emplace} & like \texttt{emplace}, but arguments untouched on a hit & preserves move-only arguments \\
\texttt{map::insert\_\allowbreak or\_\allowbreak assign} & ordered search + insert or assign & overwrites (unlike \texttt{insert}) \\
\texttt{map::count} / \texttt{contains} / \texttt{equal\_range} & reduce to \texttt{find} resp.\ \texttt{lower\_bound}+\texttt{upper\_bound} & always 0 or 1 for a unique-key map \\
\texttt{vector::}\allowbreak\texttt{push\_back} / \texttt{emplace\_back} & possible growth (realloc + relocation) + construction at the end & hidden cost + invalidation on realloc \\
\texttt{vector::insert} / \texttt{emplace} & possible growth + tail shift + insertion & extra $O(n)$ shift \\
\texttt{vector::resize} & append (default/value) or truncate the tail & bidirectional; does not shrink capacity \\
\texttt{vector::assign} & \texttt{clear} + insert range/$n\times$ & capacity reused where possible \\
\bottomrule
\end{longtable}

\noindent Functions that both shells \emph{share} by name --- \texttt{size}, \texttt{empty}, \texttt{clear},
the iterator families, \texttt{swap}, \texttt{get\_allocator}, and \texttt{insert}/\texttt{emplace}/%
\texttt{erase} --- carry key semantics in the map and position/index semantics in the vector;
\texttt{operator[]}, for instance, inserts in the map but is an unchecked index access in the vector. The exact
cache-engine-internal function-handle hops behind these contracts are documented in a dedicated cache-engine
developer document.
