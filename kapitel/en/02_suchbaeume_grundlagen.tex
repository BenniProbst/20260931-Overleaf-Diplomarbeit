hashing, spatial and flat structures --- and depict \emph{every} known algorithm visually in detail. Here the
abstraction is already located conceptually: every algorithm is a point in a shared design space.

Search structures can be classified by the mechanism with which they map a key to a
record~\cite{knuth1998taocp3}. \emph{Comparison-based} structures locate the key via order comparisons
and presuppose a total order --- from the self-balancing AVL~\cite{adelsonvelsky1962avl} and red-black
trees~\cite{bayer1972symmetric,guibas1978redblack} through the block- and external-memory-oriented
B/B\textsuperscript{+} trees~\cite{bayer1972btree,comer1979btree}, whose nodes typically correspond to a
cache or memory block size~\cite{graefe2001btree,hankins2003nodesize}, to the main-memory-oriented
T-tree~\cite{lehman1986ttree}, which combines node-local value ranges with AVL balancing and thereby
reduces the number of pointer indirections per search step. \emph{Digital} structures, by contrast, branch on the bytes of the key
rather than by comparison, so that the search-path length depends on the key length and not on the
number of keys: the \emph{trie}~\cite{delabriandais1959trie,fredkin1960trie} as the basic form, in which every root
path spells out a key and common prefixes are shared, the
path-compressed radix/PATRICIA trees~\cite{morrison1968patricia} that collapse sparse one-child chains,
and the \emph{Adaptive Radix Tree}
(ART)~\cite{leis2013art}, which adapts the node representation to the actual fanout and serves throughout
this thesis as the reference design.

Beyond the tree-like methods, \emph{hashing} maps keys directly via a hash function and has no inherent
order, which makes range queries expensive~\cite{knuth1998taocp3}; cuckoo hashing~\cite{pagh2004cuckoo}
and SIMD-accelerated tables such as SwissTable~\cite{abseil_swisstable} stand alongside this work as a
non-tree-like baseline. \emph{Spatial} structures (k-d tree~\cite{bentley1975kdtree},
quadtree~\cite{finkel1974quadtree}, R-tree~\cite{guttman1984rtree}) partition a multidimensional key
space and lie outside the scope, as do the theoretical \enquote{exotics} --- the van Emde Boas
tree~\cite{vanemdeboas1975} and the y-fast trie~\cite{willard1983yfast} with $O(\log\log u)$ over a
universe of size~$u$, or the Fusion Tree~\cite{fredman1993fusion} that undercuts the comparison bound ---
as well as the flat, sorted array with binary or interpolation search~\cite{knuth1998taocp3} --- strictly speaking the single leaf
of a tree --- and its
string generalization, the suffix array~\cite{manzini2002suffixarray}. This thesis concentrates on the
tree-like structures of the comparison- and digital-based classes; the principle developed here is,
however, not bound to trees, for as outwardly different as the classes appear, they share a common inner
\emph{anatomy} of orthogonal partial decisions that Section~\ref{sec:software-means} exposes as an axis
set (Figure~\ref{fig:search-map}).

Orthogonal to this search-algorithm view stands the established taxonomy of the \emph{containers} of the
C++ standard library~\cite{iso_cpp}: sequence containers (\texttt{std::vector}) order by insertion
position, ordered-associative ones (\texttt{std::map}, \texttt{std::set}) by key order, unordered ones
(\texttt{std::unordered\_map}) map via a hash function. These standardized interface contracts form the
common basis against which structurally different methods can be measured uniformly --- the ordered map
as the common denominator of comparison- and digital-based search structures, the \texttt{std::vector} as
the sequence view, and the hash table as the unordered counter-check; their placement in the conceptual
framework of this thesis (genus, genus subclass, axis) is developed in Section~\ref{sec:software-means}.

The design space thus outlined is measured systematically in the state of the art. The analysis draws on
33~deeply analysed search-algorithm publications (P01--P33) and a parallel allocator corpus of 23~works
(A01--A23), whose axis assignment is each persisted as an XML profile in the \texttt{comdare-cache-engine};
the complete building-block and allocator matrices are given in the appendix. The core contribution is
the \emph{dissection}: every overall algorithm --- a \enquote{living being} --- is decomposed into its
orthogonal organs, the axes, and the organ algorithms thus obtained are sorted \emph{per axis}, with a
brief reference to the originating living being, following a web-verified provenance map over roughly
110~organ algorithms; the systematic, axis-wise dissection takes place in Chapter~\ref{ch:measurement}
(Section~\ref{sec:sota-instances}). Every state-of-the-art algorithm thereby appears as a point in the
Cartesian product of the axes (Figure~\ref{fig:design-space}). That the axis framework also carries
non-tree-like methods is shown by a pure hash table (SwissTable~\cite{abseil_swisstable}) as a
counter-check within the SearchAlgorithm genus: it satisfies the point-query subset of the
\texttt{std::map} interface (\texttt{find}/\texttt{insert}/\texttt{erase}), occupies a limited axis subset
--- allocation, prefetch, concurrency, memory layout, and ISA-SIMD remain shared, the trie-specific axes
drop out --- and thus requires no genus of its own.

The publications stem from six thematic \emph{origin clusters} that here serve only the holistic placement
of each living being before it is decomposed into its organs. The \emph{trie family} comprises ART
(P01)~\cite{leis2013art}, HOT (P02)~\cite{binna2018hot}, Masstree (P03)~\cite{mao2012masstree}, CoCo-Trie
(P04)~\cite{boffa2024coco}, START (P05)~\cite{fent2020start}, and the succinct tries LOUDS
(P09)~\cite{jacobson1989louds} and SuRF (P10)~\cite{zhang2018surf}. The \emph{hybrid and
B\textsuperscript{+} family} unites the B\textsuperscript{2}-tree (P06)~\cite{schmeisser2022b2tree},
Wormhole (P07)~\cite{wu2019wormhole}, and the cache-sensitive CSS, CSB\textsuperscript{+}, and Hankins
trees (P11--P13)~\cite{rao1999css,rao2000csb,hankins2003nodesize}. The \emph{layout theory} ranges from
Samuel (P14)~\cite{samuel2005procaware} and Graefe/Larson (P15)~\cite{graefe2001btree} through the
cache-oblivious designs of Bender et
al.\ (P16/P17)~\cite{bender2000cobtree,bender2005coboblivious,bender2002treelayout,bender2002scanning,bender2002orderlist}
to the layout-invariant trees of Saikkonen/Soisalon-Soininen
(P18/P19)~\cite{saikkonen2008multilevel,saikkonen2016layout}. The \emph{prefetching} comprises \emph{B-Trees
Are Back} (P20)~\cite{mueller2025btreesback}, the prefetching B\textsuperscript{+} trees of Chen et
al.\ (P21/P22)~\cite{chen2001prefetch,chen2002fractal}, Khan (P23)~\cite{khan2010adaptive}, Naderan-Tahan
(P24)~\cite{naderan2016adaptivefilter}, Mahling (P25)~\cite{mahling2025hotpath}, as well as Zhang

\section{Hardware: Historical Adaptation to Search Performance}\label{sec:hardware-history}
The performance of search structures has always been driven by hardware. We introduce the historical advent of
cache-near hardware --- cache hierarchy, cache line, address translation, coherence --- focusing on the
\emph{static and compile-time-relevant} properties that later carry the abstraction.
\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\footnotesize, align=center]
  \foreach \i in {0,...,7} { \draw (\i*0.55,0) rectangle (\i*0.55+0.55,0.55); }
  \foreach \i in {0,1,2} { \fill[green!30] (\i*0.55+0.03,0.03) rectangle (\i*0.55+0.52,0.52); }
  \node[anchor=west, align=left] at (4.8,0.27){64-byte cache line:\\only 3 of 8 units used $\Rightarrow$ low utilization};
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{Cache-line utilization: a cache line is always loaded in full; if only a few of its bytes are used, effective bandwidth drops --- cache-aware layout raises utilization.}\label{fig:cache-line}
\end{figure}
Modern processors interpose several cache levels (L1, L2, and --- not on every platform --- L3) between
the registers and main memory~\cite{hennessy2019architecture}; an access that finds its datum nearby is
cheap, whereas a miss down to main memory costs several hundred cycles~\cite{drepper2007memory}, on
multi-socket systems additionally depending on the owning NUMA node. The transfer unit between the levels
is the \emph{cache line} (Figure~\ref{fig:cache-line}); its size is no universal constant but hard-wired
per microarchitecture --- 64\,bytes on x86-64 and common ARM cores, 128\,bytes on Apple Silicon L2,
narrower 32\,bytes on older or embedded
cores~\cite{hennessy2019architecture,intel_optimization,amd_sog,apple_optimization}. Programs can
\emph{read} it at runtime --- via \texttt{CPUID} (x86), \texttt{CTR\_EL0} (ARM), or \texttt{riscv\_hwprobe}
(RISC-V)~\cite{arm_arm,intel_sdm} --- but not change it; the depth of the hierarchy also varies (simple
RISC-V cores end at L2, some AMD processors bring a stacked 3D V-Cache L3). For a cache-line-aware layout,
the line size determined per target platform is therefore an \emph{input to compilation}, not a fixed
assumption --- the transfer itself is not strictly block-at-once, for \emph{critical-word-first} delivers
the requested word first and DDR5 splits the memory channel into two independent
sub-channels~\cite{micron_ddr5}.

This very cache-line-oriented layout is the historical guiding idea of the relevant research: the CSS
tree~\cite{rao1999css} introduces the cache-line node, the CSB\textsuperscript{+} tree~\cite{rao2000csb}
the contiguous sibling cluster, Hankins and Patel~\cite{hankins2003nodesize} measure the effect of node
size, and Samuel et al.~\cite{samuel2005procaware} make the node processor-aware; the Adaptive Radix
Tree~\cite{leis2013art} grades node size cache-line-orientedly with Node4/16/48/256, and Schmidt et
al.~\cite{schmidt2025hbm} measure stride versus sequential accesses on high-bandwidth memory. On the
memory-layout level there thus stand concretely the dense and the cache-line-aligned array-of-structs,
the column-oriented struct-of-arrays, the bit-packed LOUDS~\cite{jacobson1989louds}, and the SIMD-tiled
AoSoA. The multi-level hierarchy itself is moreover the subject of software/hardware-cooperative
prefetching methods such as the hierarchical instruction prefetcher of Zhang et
al.~\cite{zhang2025hierarchical}, which enters as a prefetching instance and not as a data-structure-specific
tree data prefetch.

Besides the data-cache hierarchy there exists a second locality boundary: the \emph{translation lookaside
buffer} (TLB) caches virtual-to-physical mappings, and a TLB miss triggers an expensive page
walk~\cite{hennessy2019architecture}. For locality reasons processors separate the instruction TLB from
the data TLB (dTLB) and lay it out in multiple stages~\cite{intel_sdm,drepper2007memory}; for search
structures the dTLB is the second, independent boundary alongside the cache line, since large or widely
scattered nodes raise dTLB pressure independently of cache utilization, which is why large pages (huge
pages, 2\,MiB/1\,GiB) enlarge the TLB reach. In the state of the art the dTLB appears mainly as a
\emph{measurement target}; algorithmically it is addressed by huge pages, which several allocators
(mimalloc, jemalloc, TCMalloc, snmalloc) support and which Schmidt et al.~\cite{schmidt2025hbm} use
empirically with 2-MiB pages --- a \emph{dedicated} dTLB-optimization axis nevertheless remains
underrepresented in the search-algorithm papers, an observation to which the research gap in
Chapter~\ref{ch:measurement} (Section~\ref{sec:task-concerns}) returns.

The write and update behaviour of the caches, too, is largely microarchitecturally fixed. Writes follow
\emph{write-through} or \emph{write-back}, orthogonally to which the allocation policy (write-allocate or
no-write-allocate) determines the handling of a write miss; ARMv8 makes this choice explicit via page
attributes~\cite{arm_arm}, while x86 normally uses write-back with write-allocate~\cite{intel_sdm}. Which
line is evicted is decided by a heuristic replacement strategy from LRU/pseudo-LRU to the two-bit-frugal
\emph{re-reference interval prediction}~\cite{jaleel2010rrip,qureshi2007adaptive}; how many levels hold a
line at once is governed by the inclusion policy, and in multi-core systems an invalidation-based
coherence protocol (MESI with the variants MOESI and MESIF) secures
consistency~\cite{hennessy2019architecture}. As a concrete instance of these fixed properties, the L3
inclusion policy varies between generations --- inclusive on early Intel client cores, non-inclusive on
Intel server from Skylake-SP, and predominantly victim-like on AMD~Zen~\cite{intel_optimization,amd_sog}.
Since none of this is freely selectable at runtime, it enters the system-optimized binary as a
\emph{measured} platform property; its effect --- in particular the \emph{cache-line ping-pong} under
competing writes to the same line --- motivates the telemetry and concurrency axes
(Section~\ref{sec:software-means}, Chapter~\ref{ch:impl}).

These properties span the spectrum in which a data structure can be located: \emph{cache-aware} when its
layout is tuned to concrete cache parameters, and \emph{cache-oblivious} when it achieves good locality
across all levels without knowing them~\cite{frigo1999cache}. Both philosophies are instantiated in the
state of the art: Bender et al.\ ground the cache-oblivious B-tree on the van Emde Boas
layout~\cite{bender2005coboblivious}, building on the order-maintenance/packed-memory
primitive~\cite{bender2002orderlist}, the cache-oblivious traversal of ordered sets~\cite{bender2002scanning},
and the near-optimal layout of a tree of fixed topology~\cite{bender2002treelayout,bender2000cobtree};
Saikkonen and Soisalon-Soininen maintain a cache-sensitive layout invariant even under updates, and
Graefe/Larson~\cite{graefe2001btree} compare both approaches across the CPU cache levels, while all
practical trie and B\textsuperscript{+} indexes are designed cache-aware. This thesis pursues a third,
intermediate path: the actual cache properties (line size, hierarchy depth, topology) are \emph{determined}
per target platform and enter a system-optimized binary as fixed quantities, while the \emph{configurable}
cache-near settings (prefetch distance, batching and inline thresholds, pool budgets) are permuted as axis
parameters against an already-loaded binary --- graded by what hardware and operating system allow, from
fully runtime-configurable (mutable Linux with MSR access) through compile-static (immutable operating
systems) to fixed (read-only cache registers on ARM and RISC-V). Thus cache-oblivious behaviour is
augmented with \emph{measured} locality knowledge; the \emph{why} of this construction is developed in
Section~\ref{sec:software-means}.

\section{Software Means and Synthesis into the Abstraction}\label{sec:software-means}
With which software-system means --- compile-time metaprogramming, static use of the hardware from
Section~\ref{sec:hardware-history} --- can the abstraction be \emph{realized}? Here we bring the known methods
together visually into \emph{our} axes and define all components, the conceptual special constructs (hulls,
particular design patterns), the terminology, and the foundations of scientific measurement.
The central idea is to treat an algorithm not as a monolith but as a \emph{composition of orthogonal
axes} --- each axis a partial decision of a cache-aware index (how pages are arranged, how nodes branch,
how memory is allocated). A concrete algorithm is then a point in the Cartesian product of all axes, a
state-of-the-art design an explicit configuration. The decisive property is \emph{modular
exchangeability}: a new axis variant can be studied in isolation --- its effect measured per axis \emph{and}
for the whole algorithm --- without reimplementing the others; this is precisely what solves the
separability problem, since the contribution of each constituent becomes measurable in isolation while the
other axes are held fixed. A vivid metaphor captures this: a search algorithm as a \emph{living being}
whose \emph{organs} are the axes, and the measurement system as its medical diagnostics
(Figure~\ref{fig:anatomy-bridge}).

feature composition, the anatomy to the wiring between the features. Which axis uses which is shown by the

The actual contribution thus lies less in a single structure than in a \emph{tool}: instead of solving the
comparison problem directly, this thesis first builds the \enquote{shovel} --- the permutation and
measurement apparatus --- with which arbitrary axis combinations can be generated and fairly measured.
Scientifically this apparatus situates itself in the software engineering of \emph{composition}: invasive
software composition from gray-box components~\cite{assmann2003invasive}, generative
programming~\cite{czarnecki2000generative}, and feature-oriented software product
lines~\cite{apel2013feature,batory1992hierarchical}. The axis concept itself is adopted from prior work by
the author (the Comdare\,/\,BEP~Venture~UG brand, originally for the deduplication optimization of the
UltiHash database); the contribution of this thesis is its transfer to cache-aware search structures and
its design-pattern-driven, measurement-oriented realization. In software-engineering terms the apparatus
is a composition of named design patterns and two new compile-time patterns (Figure~\ref{fig:patterns}).

The classical patterns are not ornament but the wiring of the apparatus: \emph{Strategy} encapsulates each
of the nineteen axes as an interchangeable algorithm, \emph{Abstract Factory}, \emph{Composite}, and
\emph{Iterator} build and traverse the permutation tree, \emph{Adapter} binds foreign code behind the
stable engine interface, and \emph{Observer} as well as \emph{Decorator} (non-intrusive measurement hulls)
collect the measurements, while a \emph{Memento} (copy-on-write) secures the state between warm-up and
measurement~\cite{gamma1994patterns}. Static polymorphism is provided by the \emph{Curiously Recurring
Template Pattern} (CRTP) together with \emph{Template-Method} concept checks; \texttt{if~constexpr} and
\texttt{std::conditional\_t} select per axis between the probe and the cache-engine standard, and
\emph{policy-based design} captures the composition as a tuple of axis policies~\cite{alexandrescu2001modern}. C++23 modules structure the translation units but by
themselves do not yet guarantee portable ABI stability~\cite{wg21_modules_abi} --- only the C-stable
module boundary establishes that.
Beyond the established patterns, the thesis names two constructions for which a web search found no
established term: the \emph{lazy-sparse cartesian materialization}, which counts the product space of over
$10^{14}$ axis combinations only arithmetically and materializes each path individually on demand in mixed
radix, rather than blowing up the compiler with the full type tree; and the \emph{type-driven source
emission}, which extracts fully qualified type names from a compile-time axis composition, renders them to
C++ source, and loads each permutation as a standalone module across the ABI boundary --- thus the probe
PRT-ART is compiled, by metaprogramming, in matrix layers against the cache engine.
This one architecture is made visible to the outside through an ABI-stable \emph{hull}. The living being
\texttt{SearchAlgorithm} and its ABI-runtime view --- the \enquote{SearchEngine} --- are merely two views
of the same entity across the module boundary, not a parallel construct: the layering
\texttt{CacheEngine}\,$\to$\,\texttt{ExecutionEngine}\,$\to$\,\texttt{SearchEngine} separates the roles ---
the \texttt{CacheEngine} as the axis building-block library, the \texttt{ExecutionEngine} as the root of
the execution and measurement tooling (OS primitives; in experiment mode the measurement aggregator over
exactly one observer interface), and the \texttt{SearchEngine} as the search-API view measured over
precisely that interface (Figure~\ref{fig:one-architecture}). Axis-less \emph{viruses} stand as siblings
beneath the same root but carry no anatomy.

To compare structurally different search algorithms \emph{fairly}, the thesis maps them all onto a single,
well-understood interface: the C++ contract of the ordered map \texttt{std::map<Key,\,Value>} with point
query (\texttt{find}), insertion/update (\texttt{insert}, \texttt{emplace}),
deletion (\texttt{erase}), and order-based navigation (\texttt{lower\_bound}/\texttt{upper\_bound}, range
iteration)~\cite{iso_cpp}. The axes describe the \emph{inner} behaviour behind this fixed interface, so
that the comparison of two conformant \texttt{std::map} implementations becomes the central measurement
act; in parallel the sequence hull \texttt{std::vector} (indexed access, resizing, contiguous storage)
serves as a container comparison case, to check whether an axis composite also carries the container and
not only the search semantics. Behind the fixed outer interface the system distinguishes several
\emph{levels of dynamics} that determine \emph{when} an axis decision is made: the topmost split of compile
time versus run time; the living-being type, which occupies only a subset of the axes; the axis algorithms
compiled into a binary at compile time (static recombination); the dynamic sub-axes, whose runtime
parameters (prefetch distance, batch size, inline threshold, pool budget) are varied without
recompilation; and the static sub-axes as a compile-time choice among partial algorithms of differing
complexity. Against the static design-space enumeration of the \emph{Data
Calculator}~\cite{idreos2018datacalculator} and classical \emph{policy-based
design}~\cite{alexandrescu2001modern}, this taxonomy expressly carries compile- and runtime-variable axis
levels together.

These building blocks are only as meaningful as their measurement. Scientific measurement is subject to
established quality criteria~\cite{hoefler2015benchmarking}: \emph{reproducibility} (fixed seeds,
deterministic order, documented configuration), \emph{validity} (internally causally isolated effects,
construct and external transferability), \emph{fairness} (identical keys, workloads, and warm-up; missing
capabilities reported as \emph{N/A} rather than covertly emulated), and \emph{dispersion} as part of the
statement (percentiles over HDR histograms instead of arithmetic means, with significance testing). On
these criteria the measurement patterns used here build: a \emph{two-phase operation loop} separates a
discarded warm-up from the actual measurement; collection is \emph{hybrid} --- isolated axis algorithms
directly against one another, a composed living being centrally over an ABI-stable per-axis observer
interface ---; and a \emph{conformance gate} checks the \texttt{std::map} contract before each measurement,
so that only comparable things are compared. These concepts form the \emph{definitional classes} of
measurement; their technical \emph{instances} --- hardware performance counters, OTF2/VAMPIR profiling, the
TU-Dresden hardware measurement methodology --- and the \emph{why} of their choice are developed by the
measurement system in Chapter~\ref{ch:measurement}.

What these measurement patterns concretely collect are three opposing components --- \emph{latency},
\emph{throughput} and \emph{memory footprint} ---, against which every axis permutation spans as a
trade-off profile: the same permutation that is a point in the design space in
Figure~\ref{fig:design-space} appears here as a point inside the trade-off triangle
(Figure~\ref{fig:measurement-triangle}). A fourth component, the one actually central for cache-aware
search structures --- the cache-aware behaviour at the node, data and index level via hardware
performance counters --- is not yet collected in the current implementation state (PMC capture is off by
default) and therefore remains marked as a pending fourth measurement component.
