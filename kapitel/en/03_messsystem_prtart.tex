Building on the foundations from Chapter~\ref{ch:foundations}, this chapter introduces the actual concerns of the
task and develops as their \emph{solution} the anatomy model as the solution concept and the measurement
system built upon it --- demonstrated on the probe PRT-ART\@.

\section{Concerns of the Task Assignment}\label{sec:task-concerns}
The task demands decomposing and measuring cache-aware search algorithms such that the contribution of each design
decision becomes visible in isolation. We make these concerns fully understandable here within the text --- without
recourse to the task sheet --- in the context of the foundations from Chapter~\ref{ch:foundations}.
At its core the task demands four steps: to \emph{decompose} cache-aware search algorithms into
interchangeable design constituents, to \emph{reconstruct} state-of-the-art designs as explicit
configurations, to \emph{measure} their cache-line-aware behaviour both per constituent and for the whole
algorithm on a common basis, and from this to derive the best overall algorithm and \emph{deliver} it
system-optimized. The cache-aware focus is no side condition but the guiding quantity: not asymptotic
complexity but cache-line behaviour under real load decides performance (Chapter~\ref{ch:foundations}), and
precisely this behaviour is to become measurable per axis in isolation.

This decomposition claim is illustrated by the \emph{design space}: each of these partial decisions ---
say node type, memory layout, or allocator --- spans an axis of its own, and every state-of-the-art
algorithm is a point in the Cartesian product of these axes (Figure~\ref{fig:design-space}).

The state of the art (Chapter~\ref{ch:foundations}) thereby reveals the central gap: each of the studied
works optimizes only a few axes in isolation, and in the studied corpus \emph{not a single method occupies
all nineteen axes together}. Some axes are filled exclusively with baseline or textbook variants (the
mapping between algorithm and cache traversal), others appear only as a \emph{measurement target} (the
dTLB), the spatial search class is missing entirely, and axes such as I/O, migration, filtering, and
buffering are each occupied individually but never together with the search axes in one design.

From the juxtaposition of cache-aware and cache-oblivious designs (Section~\ref{sec:hardware-history})
the thesis derives a third, intermediate path: the actual cache properties (line size, hierarchy depth,
topology) are \emph{determined} per target platform and enter a system-optimized binary as fixed
quantities, while the \emph{configurable} cache-near settings (prefetch distance, batching and inline
thresholds, pool budgets) are permuted as axis parameters against an already-loaded binary --- graded by
what hardware and operating system allow, from fully runtime-configurable (mutable Linux with MSR access)
through compile-static (immutable operating systems) to fixed (read-only cache registers on ARM and
RISC-V). Thus cache-oblivious behaviour is augmented with \emph{measured} locality knowledge.

Added to this is a methodological gap: results are reported in incomparable settings, and the frequent
choice of scenario-specific, often favourably tailored load profiles hampers a fair cross-comparison. What
is missing is a bias-reduced measurement of \emph{all} load profiles against \emph{all} methods on a common
basis --- and what this thesis contributes is therefore the systematic, orthogonal axis decomposition of
these designs together with a \emph{permuted measurement architecture} that reconstructs them as explicit
configurations and measures them on a common basis; for cache-aware algorithms cannot be differentiated
without a separation of the properties that decisively govern caching. Which of the catalogued instances
the framework adopts and which it replaces as obsolete is developed dialectically by the following
Section~\ref{sec:sota-instances} (thesis--antithesis--synthesis); the solution concept
(Section~\ref{sec:anatomy-model}) and the measurement system built upon it
(Section~\ref{sec:measurement-system}) are the solution that closes this gap.

\emph{adopted} (thesis) or an obsolete assumption is \emph{replaced} (antithesis), from which the own
contribution emerges as synthesis. The guiding idea is the \enquote{third way} from
Section~\ref{sec:task-concerns} --- measured locality knowledge instead of hard-wired or ignored cache
awareness ---, which makes each axis explicit as an orthogonal, permutable, and individually measurable

regarding the decision as selectable at all; the synthesis then consists in making it an explicit axis in

The systematic core is the axis-wise \emph{dissection}: every overall algorithm is decomposed into its
organs, and per axis the contributing organ algorithms are listed, with a brief reference to the
originating living being. The framework carries \emph{nineteen composition axes} (T0--T18) as well as
three \emph{build axes} (page type, SIMD extension, hardware profile); many axes are refined into
sub-axes (e.g.\ SA1--SA4 or AA1--AA7) and provide other axes with distributed interfaces for their detail
operations. Table~\ref{tab:axes-overview} gives the overview of which publications contribute an organ per
axis; the thematic origin clusters of the prior works thereby appear \emph{dissolved} into their organ
contributions.

The \emph{search axis} (T0) carries the most organs --- as original bindings ART~\cite{leis2013art},
HOT~\cite{binna2018hot}, START~\cite{fent2020start}, SuRF~\cite{zhang2018surf}, and
Wormhole~\cite{wu2019wormhole}, plus generic search methods such as k-ary search~\cite{schlegel2009kary},
interpolation search~\cite{perl1978interpolation}, Eytzinger-layout search~\cite{khuong2017eytzinger},
skip list~\cite{pugh1990skiplist}, B-tree~\cite{bayer1972btree}, and open-addressing
hashing~\cite{knuth1998taocp3}. The traversal axes separate the
algorithm and cache views: T1 ranges from cache-line walking through stride prefetch~\cite{chen2001prefetch}
and HBM proximity~\cite{schmidt2025hbm} to cache-oblivious recursion~\cite{frigo1999cache}, while T2
(mapping) remains a sparsely occupied axis via linear 1:1 mapping, hash redirect~\cite{wu2019wormhole}, and
permutation index~\cite{mao2012masstree}. The \emph{path compression} (T3) instantiates the byte-wise
variant~\cite{leis2013art}, the Patricia split~\cite{morrison1968patricia,binna2018hot}, the macro
node~\cite{boffa2024coco}, and the multi-byte rewiring~\cite{fent2020start}, and the \emph{node-type axis}
(T4) bundles the adaptive layouts from Node4/16/48/256~\cite{leis2013art} through HOT
compound~\cite{binna2018hot} and the CSS/CSB\textsuperscript{+}/Hankins nodes to the PRT-ART family.

The \emph{memory-layout axis} (T5) ranges from the cache-line-aligned AoS through pointer-free
arrangement~\cite{rao1999css}, sibling cluster~\cite{rao2000csb}, and embedded
tree~\cite{schmeisser2022b2tree} to the bit-packed LOUDS~\cite{jacobson1989louds}. The \emph{allocator
axis} (T6) is the most densely occupied with the corpus A01--A23 and is refined into seven sub-axes ---
from free-list topology through thread locality/NUMA affinity~\cite{ungethuem2017survey} and the allocation
strategy to reclamation (epoch, RCU~\cite{mckenney2001rcu}, hazard pointers~\cite{michael2004hazard},
QSBR); the code provides 25 allocator implementations for the 23-work corpus (individual works contribute
several variants), of which Table~\ref{tab:allocator-profiles} lists
the ten runnable profiles with license and workload affinity. The \emph{value axis} (T11) distinguishes
inline value~\cite{leis2013art}, external pool~\cite{wu2019wormhole}, versioned pointer~\cite{mao2012masstree},
and immutable shared ref~\cite{mckenney2001rcu}, the \emph{serialization axis} (T9) raw binary, succinct
LOUDS/FST, and varint~\cite{leis2013art}.

On the hardware-near side, the \emph{prefetch axis} (T7) ranges from no prefetch through
software-fixed~\cite{chen2001prefetch}, adaptively distanced~\cite{khan2010adaptive}, and
fill-buffer-aware~\cite{mahling2025hotpath} to path-oriented~\cite{zhang2024pathprefix} and hierarchical
bundle prefetch~\cite{zhang2025hierarchical}; the explicit hardware prefetch instruction stems from
Wormhole~\cite{wu2019wormhole}. The \emph{ISA axis} (T12) and the build SIMD extension carry the vendor
specifications (x86-64, AArch64, Power, RISC-V with SSE2/AVX2/AVX-512, NEON/SVE2, RVV). Two cross-cutting
strategy axes record what an algorithm actively \emph{uses} and how it \emph{distributes} work --- the
hardware strategy and the scheduling strategy ---, which Table~\ref{tab:hw-sched} assigns per paper. The
\emph{telemetry axis} (T10) instantiates density tracker~\cite{leis2013art}, insert
counter~\cite{binna2018hot}, HdrHistogram, and path-read counter~\cite{zhang2024pathprefix}; the
coherence-friendly leaf-only variants go back to Kuehn~\cite{kuehn2023bplustree}, while the counter in all
nodes counts as an \emph{anti-pattern} (cache-line ping-pong).

The \emph{concurrency axis} (T8) splits into pattern and locking mode: from the serial zero point through
coarse locking~\cite{dijkstra1965concurrent}, reader/writer locks~\cite{courtois1971readers}, and
optimistic lock coupling~\cite{leis2016olc} to lock-free~\cite{michael1996queue},
wait-free~\cite{herlihy1991waitfree}, as well as RCU~\cite{mckenney2001rcu} and
hazard-pointer~\cite{michael2004hazard} methods. The \emph{I/O axis} (T14) instantiates
buffered~\cite{stonebraker1981os}, direct, and memory-mapped I/O~\cite{crotty2022mmap}, the
\emph{migration axis} (T15) hot/cold separation and multi-tier RAM$\to$NVM$\to$SSD migration, and the
\emph{buffering axes} (T17/T18) range from the Disruptor ring buffer~\cite{thompson2011disruptor} through
the Bw-tree delta chain~\cite{levandoski2013bwtree}, the copy-on-write buffer~\cite{driscoll1989persistent},
and the tombstone buffer~\cite{oneil1996lsm} to lock-free SPSC~\cite{lamport_spsc} and MPMC
queues~\cite{michael1996queue}. The \emph{index-organization axis} (T13) distinguishes clustered
arrangement~\cite{bayer1972btree}, heap storage, and non-clustered secondary index~\cite{comer1979btree},
the \emph{filter axis} (T16) the Bloom~\cite{bloom1970}, Cuckoo~\cite{fan2014cuckoo}, SuRF~\cite{zhang2018surf},
and Xor filter~\cite{graf2020xor}.
The \emph{loads}, too, appear as instances. The workloads used in the literature stem from \emph{several},
mutually incomparable benchmark frameworks; Table~\ref{tab:wl-frameworks} maps them to the using

SPEC~CPU~\cite{spec_cpu2017}, the scale-out suite CloudSuite~\cite{ferdman2012cloudsuite}, and --- for the

Since these frameworks produce incomparable loads, the thesis abstracts them into a common set of load

The state of the art dissected here rests on a fixed corpus: the analysis draws on 33~deeply analysed
search-algorithm publications (P01--P33) and a parallel allocator corpus of 23~works (A01--A23), whose
axis assignment is each persisted as an XML profile in the \texttt{comdare-cache-engine}; the complete
building-block and allocator matrices are given in the appendix. The axis-wise dissection of this corpus
follows a web-verified provenance map over roughly 110~organ algorithms; every
state-of-the-art algorithm thereby appears as a point in the
Cartesian product of the axes (Figure~\ref{fig:design-space}). That the axis framework also carries
non-tree-like methods is shown by a pure hash table as a counter-check within the SearchAlgorithm
genus --- as two selectable T0 building blocks, of which exactly one is chosen per permutation and
both of which are permuted: an open-addressing hash after Knuth (S14, implemented) and the
SwissTable~\cite{abseil_swisstable} (envisioned as a building block of its own). The counter-check
satisfies the point-query subset of the
\texttt{std::map} interface (\texttt{find}/\texttt{insert}/\texttt{erase}), occupies a limited axis subset
--- allocation, prefetch, concurrency, memory layout, and ISA-SIMD remain shared, the trie-specific axes
drop out --- and thus requires no genus of its own.

The publications stem from six thematic \emph{origin clusters} that here serve only the holistic placement
of each living being before it is decomposed into its organs. The \emph{trie family} comprises ART
(P01)~\cite{leis2013art}, HOT (P02)~\cite{binna2018hot}, Masstree (P03)~\cite{mao2012masstree}, CoCo-Trie
(P04)~\cite{boffa2024coco}, START (P05)~\cite{fent2020start}, and the succinct tries LOUDS

B\textsuperscript{+} family} unites the B\textsuperscript{2}-tree (P06)~\cite{schmeisser2022b2tree},
Wormhole (P07)~\cite{wu2019wormhole}, and the cache-sensitive CSS, CSB\textsuperscript{+}, and Hankins
trees (P11--P13)~\cite{rao1999css,rao2000csb,hankins2003nodesize}. The \emph{layout theory} ranges from
Samuel (P14)~\cite{samuel2005procaware} and Graefe/Larson (P15)~\cite{graefe2001btree} through the
cache-oblivious designs of Bender et
al.\ (P16/P17)~\cite{bender2000cobtree,bender2005coboblivious,bender2002treelayout,bender2002scanning,bender2002orderlist}
to the layout-invariant trees of Saikkonen/Soisalon-Soininen
(P18/P19)~\cite{saikkonen2008multilevel,saikkonen2016layout}. The \emph{prefetching} comprises \emph{B-Trees
Are Back} (P20)~\cite{mueller2025btreesback}, the prefetching B\textsuperscript{+} trees of Chen et
al.\ (P21/P22)~\cite{chen2001prefetch,chen2002fractal}, Khan (P23)~\cite{khan2010adaptive}, Naderan-Tahan
(P24)~\cite{naderan2016adaptivefilter}, Mahling (P25)~\cite{mahling2025hotpath}, as well as Zhang

\emph{TU-Dresden line}, finally, Ungethüm (P31)~\cite{ungethuem2017survey}, Schmidt

The core finding of this load-profile analysis is a systematic \emph{bias}: each publication chooses the
load profile under which its own design performs best. Static, read-only-oriented tries and filters frame
\enquote{build-once, 100\,\% positive lookups} and avoid updates; compressed methods make the negative-query
share the primary axis; zipfian-centred works favour cache-aware reordering layouts; and write- or
concurrency-heavy works conversely compete only where static structures fail. Fourteen of the publications
provide no index load profile of their own at all. Only when \emph{all} load profiles run against
\emph{all} living-being binaries --- the workload as a dynamic axis (Chapter~\ref{ch:eval}) --- can the
\enquote{winner only in its self-chosen home profile} be made visible and bias-reduced comparability be
gained.

Finally, the quality criteria of scientific measurement (cf.\ the definitional classes in Section~\ref{sec:measurement-system}), too, appear as
concrete technologies.\ \emph{Hardware performance counters} (PMC, via \texttt{perf})~\cite{demelo2010perf}
are nearly universal and deliver cache-miss rates, dTLB misses, branch misses, and IPC/CPI\@; \emph{profilers}
such as OTF2/VAMPIR~\cite{berthold2023vampir} and VTune~\cite{reinders2005vtune} collect trace-based runtime
profiles, \emph{simulators} such as gem5~\cite{binkert2011gem5} provide hardware-accurate estimates where
real counters are absent, and the \emph{HdrHistogram}~\cite{tene_hdrhistogram} collects the latency
percentiles (p50/p95/p99). On the collection the evaluation builds: instead of arithmetic means, median and
percentiles are reported, differences tested for significance with the distribution-free
\emph{Mann-Whitney U test}~\cite{mann1947utest} (with Holm correction~\cite{holm1979sequential}) and
quantified via the effect measure \emph{Cliff's~$\delta$}~\cite{cliff1993dominance}, following the
recommendations for scientific benchmarking~\cite{hoefler2015benchmarking}. A methodological line of its own
is the hardware-near measurement methodology of TU Dresden (Habich group): Ungethüm et
al.~\cite{ungethuem2017survey} systematize the hardware optimizations for database machines, Schmidt et
al.~\cite{schmidt2025hbm} measure stride versus sequential accesses on HBM, and VAMPIR~\cite{berthold2023vampir}
profiles non-functional properties of heterogeneous memory. From all these technologies follows the set of
measurement categories --- cache-line utilization, cache misses, dTLB misses, memory footprint, branch
misses, IPC/CPI, latency, throughput, and energy ---, whose time- and observer-based subset the present
apparatus already resolves, while the counter-based PMC categories remain bound to hardware-counter access;
the complete measurement architecture is developed by the measurement system
(Section~\ref{sec:measurement-system}) and Chapter~\ref{ch:eval}.

\section{Solution Concept: the Anatomy Model}\label{sec:anatomy-model}
From the dialectical adoption of the state of the art emerges the solution concept of this thesis; this
section develops it as the \emph{anatomy model} and in doing so defines the terminology and conceptual
special constructs that carry the following sections.

\subsection{Axis Composition and the Living-Being Metaphor}\label{sec:anatomy-metaphor}
The central idea is to treat an algorithm not as a monolith but as a \emph{composition of orthogonal
axes} --- each axis a partial decision of a cache-aware index (how pages are arranged, how nodes branch,
how memory is allocated). A concrete algorithm is then a point in the Cartesian product of all axes, a
state-of-the-art design an explicit configuration. The decisive property is \emph{modular
exchangeability}: a new axis variant can be studied in isolation --- its effect measured per axis \emph{and}
for the whole algorithm --- without reimplementing the others; this is precisely what solves the
separability problem, since the contribution of each constituent becomes measurable in isolation while the
other axes are held fixed. A vivid metaphor captures this: a search algorithm as a \emph{living being}
whose \emph{organs} are the axes, and the measurement system as its medical diagnostics
(Figure~\ref{fig:anatomy-bridge}).

\subsection{The Three-Level Model and the Three Genera}\label{sec:anatomy-levels}
These organs are embedded in \emph{one} architecture --- a single, self-contained hierarchy without
parallel trees. At its root stands the \texttt{ExecutionEngine} as the common, measurable abstraction over
everything measurable; beneath it three levels can be distinguished: the \emph{genus} as the
outward-visible algorithm interface (the \emph{probe dock}), beneath it the \emph{genus subclass} with a
fixed axis set, beneath that the \emph{organs} themselves (Figure~\ref{fig:three-levels}).
\enquote{Living being} and \texttt{SearchAlgorithm} denote the same entity --- metaphor and technical term
---; the anatomy is not a second model but the \emph{body} of this living being: the fixed composition of
its nineteen axis-organs.

\subsection{Design Patterns and Compile-Time Patterns}\label{sec:anatomy-patterns}
The actual contribution thus lies less in a single structure than in a \emph{tool}: instead of solving the
comparison problem directly, this thesis first builds the \enquote{shovel} --- the permutation and
measurement apparatus --- with which arbitrary axis combinations can be generated and fairly measured.
Scientifically this apparatus situates itself in the software engineering of \emph{composition}: invasive
software composition from gray-box components~\cite{assmann2003invasive}, generative
programming~\cite{czarnecki2000generative}, and feature-oriented software product
lines~\cite{apel2013feature,batory1992hierarchical}. The axis concept itself is adopted from prior work by
the author (the Comdare\,/\,BEP~Venture~UG brand, originally for the deduplication optimization of the
UltiHash database); the contribution of this thesis is its transfer to cache-aware search structures and
its design-pattern-driven, measurement-oriented realization. In software-engineering terms the apparatus
is a composition of named design patterns and two new compile-time patterns (Figure~\ref{fig:patterns}).

The classical patterns are not ornament but the wiring of the apparatus: \emph{Strategy} encapsulates each
of the nineteen axes as an interchangeable algorithm, \emph{Abstract Factory}, \emph{Composite}, and
\emph{Iterator} build and traverse the permutation tree, \emph{Adapter} binds foreign code behind the
stable engine interface, and \emph{Observer} as well as \emph{Decorator} (non-intrusive measurement hulls)
collect the measurements, while a \emph{Memento} (copy-on-write) secures the state between warm-up and
measurement~\cite{gamma1994patterns}. Static polymorphism is provided by the \emph{Curiously Recurring
Template Pattern} (CRTP) together with \emph{Template-Method} concept checks; concept-checked
\texttt{if~constexpr} (\texttt{resolve\_baustein}) selects per axis between the probe and the cache-engine standard, and
\emph{policy-based design} captures the composition as a tuple of axis policies~\cite{alexandrescu2001modern}. C++23 modules structure the translation units but by
themselves do not yet guarantee portable ABI stability~\cite{wg21_modules_abi} --- only the C-stable
module boundary establishes that.
Beyond the established patterns, the thesis names two constructions for which a web search found no
established term: the \emph{lazy-sparse cartesian materialization}, which counts the product space of over
$10^{14}$ axis combinations only arithmetically and materializes each path individually on demand in mixed
radix, rather than blowing up the compiler with the full type tree; and the \emph{type-driven source
emission}, which extracts fully qualified type names from a compile-time axis composition, renders them to
C++ source, and loads each permutation as a standalone module across the ABI boundary --- thus the probe
PRT-ART is compiled, by metaprogramming, in matrix layers against the cache engine.

How this \emph{one} architecture becomes visible in the code through an ABI-stable hull is deepened in
Chapter~\ref{ch:impl} (Section~\ref{sec:axes-impl}); first, the following section turns to the question of
how the living beings thus composed can be measured fairly.

\section{The Measurement System}\label{sec:measurement-system}
To compare structurally different search algorithms \emph{fairly}, the thesis maps them all onto a single,
well-understood interface: the C++ contract of the ordered map \texttt{std::map<Key,\,Value>} with point
query (\texttt{find}), insertion/update (\texttt{insert}, \texttt{emplace}),
deletion (\texttt{erase}), and order-based navigation (\texttt{lower\_bound}/\texttt{upper\_bound}, range
iteration)~\cite{iso_cpp}. The axes describe the \emph{inner} behaviour behind this fixed interface, so
that the comparison of two conformant \texttt{std::map} implementations becomes the central measurement
act; in parallel the sequence hull \texttt{std::vector} (indexed access, resizing, contiguous storage)
serves as a container comparison case, to check whether an axis composite also carries the container and
not only the search semantics. Behind the fixed outer interface the system distinguishes several
\emph{levels of dynamics} that determine \emph{when} an axis decision is made: the topmost split of compile
time versus run time; the living-being type, which occupies only a subset of the axes; the axis algorithms
compiled into a binary at compile time (static recombination); the dynamic sub-axes, whose runtime
parameters (prefetch distance, batch size, inline threshold, pool budget) are varied without
recompilation; and the static sub-axes as a compile-time choice among partial algorithms of differing
complexity. Against the static design-space enumeration of the \emph{Data
Calculator}~\cite{idreos2018datacalculator} and classical \emph{policy-based
design}~\cite{alexandrescu2001modern}, this taxonomy expressly carries compile- and runtime-variable axis
levels together.

These building blocks are only as meaningful as their measurement. Scientific measurement is subject to
established quality criteria~\cite{hoefler2015benchmarking}: \emph{reproducibility} (fixed seeds,
deterministic order, documented configuration), \emph{validity} (internally causally isolated effects,
construct and external transferability), \emph{fairness} (identical keys, workloads, and warm-up; missing
capabilities reported as \emph{N/A} rather than covertly emulated), and \emph{dispersion} as part of the
statement (percentiles over HDR histograms instead of arithmetic means, with significance testing). On
these criteria the measurement patterns used here build: a \emph{two-phase operation loop} separates a
discarded warm-up from the actual measurement; collection is \emph{hybrid} --- isolated axis algorithms
directly against one another, a composed living being centrally over an ABI-stable per-axis observer
interface ---; and a \emph{conformance gate} checks the \texttt{std::map} contract before each measurement,
so that only comparable things are compared. These concepts form the \emph{definitional classes} of
measurement; their technical \emph{instances} --- hardware performance counters, OTF2/VAMPIR profiling, the
TU-Dresden hardware measurement methodology --- have already been introduced as concrete technologies in
Section~\ref{sec:sota-instances}.

What these measurement patterns concretely collect are three opposing components --- \emph{latency},
\emph{throughput} and \emph{memory footprint} ---, against which every axis permutation spans as a
trade-off profile: the same permutation that is a point in the design space in
Figure~\ref{fig:design-space} appears here as a point inside the trade-off triangle
(Figure~\ref{fig:measurement-triangle}). A fourth component, the one actually central for cache-aware
search structures --- the cache-aware behaviour at the node, data and index level via hardware
performance counters --- is not yet collected in the current implementation state (PMC capture is off by
default) and therefore remains marked as a pending fourth measurement component.

The three measurement series --- all executed by the \texttt{messung\_driver} via the
\texttt{ExperimentDriver} library --- are thus: \emph{series~A} (PRT-ART vs.\ state of the art) with the
modes A\_defined (8 Rank-1 profiles) and A\_full (all 30 SOTA profiles); \emph{series~B} (systematic
variation of the axis decisions all else held equal, to isolate the contribution of each axis); and
\emph{series~C} (concrete merge points between PRT-ART building blocks and cache-engine permutations, e.g.\
PRT-ART OLC + tcmalloc + ART page). Each series is collected in three granularities:
\emph{micro-benchmarking} compares each design constituent in isolation over the shared category interface,
\emph{macro-benchmarking} measures the individual \texttt{std::map} operations, and \emph{overall
benchmarking} runs the YCSB load profiles over every recombination. Precisely this three-granularity
methodology is the answer to the separability problem: by measuring each constituent first in isolation
(micro) before it appears in the operation and overall context, the contributions are separated and become
measurable in isolation instead of remaining blended in the overall throughput.

The system reduces the space thus spanned deliberately. With the extension to nineteen axes plus around
sixty-four sub-axes it grows to over $10^{14}$ permutations; three mechanisms dampen the explosion: the
\texttt{<expected\_workload>} profile filter routes only compatible workloads, the \texttt{Defined} mode
runs only the declared tuples (around 100--1000 per series, which enter the manuscript), and the
\texttt{Full} or \texttt{Full-Sampled} mode (e.g.\ every thousandth permutation) runs only on the ZIH
cluster, observing the axis compatibility reported by the platform probe.

\section{PRT-ART as Demonstrator}\label{sec:prtart-demo}

building blocks for the rest.

reuses existing SOTA building blocks via the permutation configuration. Thus the probe shows that the measurement system holds: a novel
living being arises from a few of its own organs plus the default toolkit (Figure~\ref{fig:prtart-demo}) and is measurable over the same

\section{Sharpening through Heuristics}\label{sec:heuristics}
From the measurement series one can derive --- as an outlook mechanism --- the best axis composition per load and
data-type profile and store it as a reusable profile.
\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering
\begin{tikzpicture}[font=\footnotesize,>=Stealth,thick,align=center,
  stage/.style={draw,rounded corners,fill=blue!4,minimum width=2.8cm,minimum height=1cm}]
  \node[stage] (mess) at (0,0){measurement\\{\scriptsize series A/B/C}};
  \node[stage] (profil) at (4.4,0){XML load profile\\{\scriptsize learned mapping}};
  \node[stage] (konfig) at (4.4,-2.2){configuration\\{\scriptsize best axis composition}};
  \node[stage] (filter) at (0,-2.2){workload filter\\{\scriptsize \texttt{<expected\_workload>}}};
  \draw[->] (mess)--(profil);\draw[->] (profil)--(konfig);
  \draw[->] (konfig)--(filter);\draw[->] (filter)--(mess);
\end{tikzpicture}
\caption{The heuristic loop: from the measurement series the best axis composition per load profile is learned, stored as an XML load profile, and fed back via the workload filter into the next measurement.}\label{fig:heuristic-loop}
\end{figure}
\emph{This is an outlook mechanism, not a function implemented within this thesis.} Beyond mere ranking, a
\emph{heuristic extraction} is envisioned as a follow-up phase after \textbf{Persist} that automatically
derives from the collected measurements which axis configuration is best per workload and dataset. The
intended mechanism is an ML classification in three steps: first, features are extracted per permutation
--- the load-profile and dataset features (workload type, key distribution, alphabet and prefix
characteristics) and the axis assignment ---, with the best performance measured per load pattern as the
target; over these an \emph{ML classifier} learns the mapping from load-profile/axis features to the best
configuration; and it delivers per workload/dataset class the recommended axis composition without
re-running the entire permutation space. The result is persisted as a reusable \emph{XML load profile} ---
symmetric to the measurement-series XML configurations --- and can be fed back into the
\texttt{<expected\_workload>} filter without a full re-measurement; this closes the loop between
measurement, learned heuristic, and configuration-guiding selection (Figure~\ref{fig:heuristic-loop}). This
\emph{measure $\to$ profile $\to$ config $\to$ filter} loop is the classical, established form of
\emph{empirical, measurement-driven autotuning}: it stands in the line of autotuning frameworks such as
OpenTuner~\cite{ansel2014opentuner} and the measure-in-the-loop autotuners FFTW~\cite{frigo1998fftw} and
ATLAS~\cite{whaley2001atlas}; the load-profile-driven configuration choice with workload filter finds its
database counterpart in OtterTune~\cite{vanaken2017ottertune} and the Database Tuning Advisor with workload
compression~\cite{agrawal2005autoadmin}, the online runtime-near variant in Active
Harmony~\cite{tapus2002activeharmony}; the term \enquote{software-in-the-loop} itself originates from the
X-in-the-loop validation taxonomy~\cite{tibba2016xil}. The
concrete realization of this selection and delivery mechanism remains reserved for future work
(Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion}). First, however, Chapter~\ref{ch:impl} deepens the concrete implementation
of the building blocks introduced here.
