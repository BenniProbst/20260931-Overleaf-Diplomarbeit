\section{Methodology}\label{sec:eval-method}
Three \emph{hypotheses} guide the analysis:
\begin{description}
  \item[H1 (page-type cost)] The mean access cost per page-type variant differs systematically by workload:
  dense page types win, as expected, under Zipf distribution (YCSB-A), range-optimized pages under range
  scans (YCSB-E).
  \item[H2 (code quality per source)] The code-quality score inherited from the original source repository
  correlates measurably with the achieved throughput.
  \item[H3 (inline vs.\ external vs.\ chain distribution)] The observed distribution of the three
  ValueHandle variants correlates with page density: dense pages prefer inline, sparse pages external, and
  PRT-ART heuristics ChainRef.
\end{description}

These \emph{evaluation hypotheses} are not to be confused with the identically named PRT-ART telemetry
metrics H1--H3 (Section~\ref{sec:prtart-demo}): the former are testable statements about the design
space, the latter measurement organs of the probe that, among other things, supply the data for this
test.

Phase~5 derives the YCSB workload per permutation from the profile's traversal tag
(Table~\ref{tab:workload-routing}); modules without a profile use the global default workload. The data
basis is real string datasets of differing characteristics (Table~\ref{tab:datasets}), each with a
reproducibility record of source, checksum, line count, preprocessing, and seed rule for the synthetic
miss, update, and delete keys.

The measurements target a local workstation for development verification and the TU-Dresden ZIH cluster
(Barnard CPU nodes with Intel Sapphire Rapids, Capella GPU) for the full sweeps, plus the Rank-3 platforms (Ryzen~9~9950X3D, % chktex 29
Intel~i9~14900KS, and an HBM platform). An \texttt{IPlatformProbe} (Phase~1) reports per platform the
runnable subset of axes (e.g.\ AVX-512 only where available, HBM-aware only on HBM hardware); per platform
a scalar, an AVX2, and --- on Barnard --- an AVX-512 build are
prepared, and on hybrid CPUs P and E cores are measured separately. On the production target machines,
moreover, each measurement is collected under \emph{two operating-system regimes} to separate production
fidelity from measurement depth: an \emph{immutable} operating system (Talos) mirrors production use and
enforces boot/compile-static cache settings (Section~\ref{sec:hardware-history}); a \emph{root Linux with
full hardware-counter access} (\texttt{perf}/MSR), by contrast, makes the counter-based measurement
categories (cache, dTLB, and branch misses, IPC/CPI, Section~\ref{sec:sota-instances}) and the
runtime-configurable cache-near axis parameters (prefetch distance, hardware prefetcher) accessible at all. The time- and observer-based categories
run identically under both regimes and secure comparability.

Fair comparability is secured by fixed rules: each comparison separates a common minimal mode (common
denominator: external value handles, no PRT-ART special paths) from the PRT-ART native mode; all methods
receive identical, normalized byte keys as well as identical miss, update, and delete sets, with warm-up,
randomization, and thread pinning logged per platform and workload. Missing capabilities of a baseline ---
e.g.\ range scans of a hash table --- are reported as N/A rather than covertly emulated; for every foreign
library the compiler, flags, ISA path, allocator, and commit hash are logged, and each configuration is
measured in several separate runs with percentiles determined over HDR histograms.

\section{Results and Analysis}\label{sec:eval-results}
At the time of writing, methodology and evaluation pipeline are in place and verified on sample data; a
first real smoke measurement series (43 permutations, real DLL measurement on the development machine) is
documented in Appendix~\ref{app:measurements} together with its limitations
(Section~\ref{sec:measurements:limitations}), the complete runs of the mandatory measurement series on
the target platforms are pending (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}). Per series run, the
\texttt{messung\_driver} writes under \texttt{Code/\_runs/<date>/<spec\_id>/} the files
\texttt{measurements.csv} --- one row per permutation with the columns of the canonical measurement
record: \texttt{op\_count}, \texttt{total\_cycles}, L1/L2/L3 cache misses, dTLB misses, coherence
invalidations, energy, plus the memory fields (\texttt{bytes\_allocated},
\texttt{bytes\_in\_use\_peak}, fragmentation); branch misses and throughput are added as a schema
extension of the record (throughput so far exists in the command path) ---,
\texttt{measurements.json}, and optional binary records; the evaluation enriches them via
\texttt{binary\_to\_csv}, renders \texttt{csv\_to\_latex} fact sheets per permutation ID (in series
\texttt{A\_defined} this corresponds to one per SOTA profile) and
\texttt{diagram\_generator} diagrams (A4-aware TikZ bar charts, scatter plots, heatmaps), which the
manuscript includes via \texttt{\textbackslash{}input\{tikz/<spec\_id>/\ldots\}} and
\texttt{\textbackslash{}input\{tabellen/<spec\_id>\ldots\}} from the language-neutral directories. The three
measurement series are then evaluated: series~A (PRT-ART vs.\ the state of the art, with the evaluation
hypotheses H1--H3 from Section~\ref{sec:eval-method}), series~B (systematic variation of the cache-engine
permutations, fed from the full join of stage~3) and series~C (merge/regression of old against new,
across builds; Table~\ref{tab:stage-series}).

The core of the evaluation is the \emph{axis sensitivity analysis}, which directly answers the separability
problem of the task: in the state of the art the weights of the improvements of \emph{individual} algorithm
constituents are so far not separable, because each publication demonstrates its benefit in its own,
incompatible measurement environment. By isolating each constituent as an interchangeable axis over an
otherwise constant structure, the axis framework makes the contribution of each design decision separable
--- for the first time under all-else-equal conditions --- and measurably attributable. From the records
per permutation, the analysis quantifies at the micro granularity which axis contributes most to the
performance variance; macro and whole-system benchmarking add the operation and the YCSB load-profile
level, and from the ranking across the three granularities follows the recommendation of standard
configurations per workload class. Thus the arc closes back to the task: the sensitivity analysis delivers
exactly the separated weighting of the individual constituents whose absence was the original problem. The
results are finally discussed against the thesis's leitmotif --- the transition from heuristic to informed
cache management, i.e.\ the question whether telemetry-driven, automatically adapted configurations
outperform statically chosen ones. Chapter~\ref{ch:conclusion} draws the conclusion from this.
